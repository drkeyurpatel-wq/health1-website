Dialysis vs Kidney Transplant: How to Think About the Choice

Medically reviewed by Dr. Krunal Patel, Urologist & Renal Transplant Surgeon · Updated July 13, 2026
Dialysis and transplant are the two treatments for kidney failure. Dialysis filters the blood by machine several times a week and is available to almost everyone. A transplant restores far more natural kidney function and generally offers better quality of life and survival — but requires a suitable donor and lifelong medication, and not everyone is a candidate.

What Each Treatment Involves

Dialysis does the work of failed kidneys artificially — haemodialysis at a centre several times a week, or peritoneal dialysis done at home. A transplant replaces the failed kidney with a healthy donor kidney, restoring continuous, natural filtering. One is an ongoing process; the other is a one-time operation followed by lifelong medication.

Quality of Life and Survival

For suitable candidates, transplant generally allows a freer life — fewer dietary and fluid restrictions, no fixed dialysis schedule — and is associated with longer survival. Dialysis is life-sustaining and the right choice for those who are not transplant candidates or who prefer it.

Who Is a Candidate for Transplant?

Transplant suitability depends on overall health, especially heart fitness, absence of active infection or cancer, and availability of a compatible donor. Some people start on dialysis and move to transplant later; others are not medically suited to transplant and remain on dialysis. A transplant team assessment gives the clearest answer.

Common Questions

Can you live a normal life on dialysis?

Many people live for years on dialysis and maintain work and family life, though it involves a fixed treatment schedule and dietary and fluid restrictions. Quality of life varies from person to person.

Is transplant always better than dialysis?

For suitable candidates it usually offers better quality of life and survival — but it requires a donor and lifelong immunosuppression, and not everyone is medically eligible. The best option is individual.
