Stroke Warning Signs: The FAST Test and Why Minutes Matter

Medically reviewed by Dr. Keyur Patel, Interventional Neurologist · Updated July 7, 2026
A stroke is diagnosed using the FAST test: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency. If you notice any one of these suddenly, call emergency services immediately — treatment within the first few hours dramatically improves the chance of full recovery.

What the FAST Test Actually Checks

FAST is a simple screening test paramedics and doctors use worldwide because it catches the majority of strokes in under a minute, without any equipment.

  • F — Face: Ask the person to smile. Does one side droop?
  • A — Arms: Ask them to raise both arms. Does one arm drift downward?
  • S — Speech: Ask them to repeat a simple sentence. Is speech slurred or strange?
  • T — Time: If any of the above is present, note the time symptoms started and call emergency services immediately.

Why "Time to Call Emergency" Is the Most Important Letter

During an ischemic stroke (the most common type), brain cells die at a rate of roughly 1.9 million per minute until blood flow is restored. Treatments like clot-dissolving medication and mechanical thrombectomy (physically removing the clot via catheter) are most effective within the first few hours of symptom onset — this window is often called the "golden hour," though the effective window can extend further for some thrombectomy candidates.

This is why calling emergency services directly — rather than waiting to see if symptoms pass, or driving yourself to a hospital — matters more than almost any other single decision in stroke care.

Other Symptoms Worth Knowing

  • Sudden numbness, especially on one side of the body
  • Sudden confusion or trouble understanding speech
  • Sudden trouble seeing in one or both eyes
  • Sudden severe headache with no known cause
  • Sudden dizziness or loss of balance and coordination

Treatment Options at a Specialized Stroke Center

Modern stroke care includes clot-dissolving medication (tPA) for eligible patients, and mechanical thrombectomy — a catheter-based procedure to physically remove a clot — for larger blockages. Interventional neurology teams also treat the underlying causes, including aneurysms and arterial stenosis, to reduce recurrence risk.

Recovery and Outcomes

Outcomes vary significantly based on stroke severity, location, and — critically — how quickly treatment begins. Patients treated within the golden hour have meaningfully better odds of returning to independent function than those treated later. This is the single biggest reason stroke awareness campaigns exist: the medical treatments are effective, but only if people recognize symptoms and act within minutes, not hours.

Common Questions

Can a stroke happen to someone young and healthy?

Yes — while risk increases with age, strokes can occur at any age, including due to underlying conditions like clotting disorders, heart defects, or uncontrolled blood pressure that may go undiagnosed in younger people.

What is a mini-stroke (TIA), and is it serious?

A TIA (transient ischemic attack) causes stroke-like symptoms that resolve within minutes to hours, but it is a serious warning sign — about 1 in 3 people who have a TIA go on to have a full stroke, often within days, making prompt evaluation essential even if symptoms have passed.

Should I drive myself to the hospital during stroke symptoms?

No — call for an ambulance instead. Emergency medical teams can begin assessment and notify the receiving hospital's stroke team before arrival, saving critical time compared to a private vehicle.
