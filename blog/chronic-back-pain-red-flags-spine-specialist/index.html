Chronic Back Pain: 7 Signs You Need to See a Spine Specialist

Medically reviewed by Dr. Munjal Shah, Spine Surgeon · Updated July 7, 2026
Most back pain improves within a few weeks with rest and conservative care. See a spine specialist promptly if you have leg weakness, numbness in the groin area, pain that worsens at night, unexplained weight loss alongside back pain, or pain following trauma — these can signal a problem that needs more than time to resolve.

The 7 Red Flags

  • Numbness, tingling, or weakness in one or both legs
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control, or numbness in the groin/inner thigh area (seek emergency care immediately)
  • Back pain that worsens at night or when lying flat
  • Back pain following a fall, accident, or other trauma
  • Unexplained weight loss alongside back pain
  • Fever combined with back pain
  • Pain that hasn't improved after 4-6 weeks of rest and conservative care

Why These Specific Signs Matter

The vast majority of back pain is mechanical — muscle strain, minor disc irritation — and improves with rest, movement, and time. The red flags above are different: they can indicate nerve compression, infection, fracture, or in rare cases something more serious, where waiting risks permanent damage rather than just prolonged discomfort.

What a Spine Evaluation Actually Involves

A spine specialist starts with a physical exam checking reflexes, strength, and sensation — not just imaging. MRI or CT scans are used selectively, based on what the exam suggests, rather than as a default first step for every case of back pain.

Treatment Doesn't Always Mean Surgery

Even among patients referred to a spine specialist, most are managed with physiotherapy, targeted injections, or activity modification rather than surgery. Surgical evaluation is reserved for cases with confirmed nerve compression causing progressive symptoms, structural instability, or red-flag findings on exam and imaging.

Common Questions

Is bed rest good for back pain?

Generally no — beyond the first day or two, staying as active as pain allows (gentle walking, normal movement) tends to produce better outcomes than prolonged bed rest for most mechanical back pain.

When is an MRI actually necessary for back pain?

MRI is typically reserved for cases with red-flag symptoms, pain persisting beyond 4-6 weeks despite conservative treatment, or when surgery is being considered — not as a routine first step for uncomplicated back pain.

Can chronic back pain be cured, or only managed?

This depends heavily on the underlying cause — some conditions (like a contained disc bulge) can resolve substantially with time and physiotherapy, while others (like significant spinal stenosis) are typically managed long-term rather than cured outright.
