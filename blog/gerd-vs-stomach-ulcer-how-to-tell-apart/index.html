GERD vs. Stomach Ulcer: How to Tell Them Apart

Medically reviewed by Dr. Sanjay Rajput, Gastroenterologist · Updated July 7, 2026
GERD (acid reflux) typically causes burning pain after meals or when lying down, often with a sour taste in the mouth, while a stomach ulcer often causes gnawing pain on an empty stomach that may briefly improve with eating. Because symptoms overlap significantly, an endoscopy is the reliable way to distinguish them.

Symptom Comparison

GERD (Acid Reflux)

  • Burning sensation rising from the stomach to the chest ("heartburn")
  • Worsens after large meals, spicy/fatty food, or lying down soon after eating
  • Sour or bitter taste in the mouth
  • Sometimes accompanied by a chronic cough or hoarseness

Stomach Ulcer

  • Gnawing or burning pain in the upper abdomen
  • Pain often on an empty stomach, sometimes improving temporarily after eating
  • May wake you at night
  • In more serious cases: dark stools or vomiting blood — seek care immediately if these occur

Why the Overlap Makes Self-Diagnosis Unreliable

Both conditions involve stomach acid and can cause burning upper-abdominal pain, which is exactly why relying on symptoms alone often leads to the wrong self-treatment. Someone with an ulcer who assumes it's "just reflux" and self-treats with antacids may delay diagnosis of an ulcer that needs specific treatment, including testing for H. pylori infection — a common ulcer cause that antacids don't address.

What an Endoscopy Actually Shows

An upper endoscopy lets a gastroenterologist directly view the esophagus, stomach, and upper small intestine, distinguishing reflux-related inflammation from an actual ulcer, and can include testing for H. pylori bacteria during the same procedure.

Treatment Differs Meaningfully

GERD is typically managed with acid-reducing medication and lifestyle changes (diet, elevation while sleeping, weight management). Ulcers, especially those caused by H. pylori, require a specific antibiotic course in addition to acid reduction — without treating the underlying cause, ulcers tend to recur.

Common Questions

Can stress cause ulcers?

Stress alone rarely causes ulcers directly, but it can worsen symptoms and may contribute alongside the two main causes: H. pylori infection and long-term use of NSAID pain relievers.

How is H. pylori tested and treated?

It can be tested via breath test, stool test, or biopsy during endoscopy, and is typically treated with a combination of antibiotics and acid-reducing medication over 1-2 weeks.

Is occasional heartburn something to worry about?

Occasional heartburn after a heavy meal is common and usually not concerning, but frequent heartburn (more than twice a week) warrants evaluation, since chronic untreated reflux can lead to esophageal complications over time.
