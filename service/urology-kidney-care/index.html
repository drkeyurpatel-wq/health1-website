Stone Treatment Without Open Surgery

Almost all kidney and ureteric stones today are treated endoscopically. PCNL removes large kidney stones through a keyhole in the back; smaller stones are managed through natural passages with no incision at all. You're typically home within a day or two, compared to weeks of recovery after old-style open stone surgery.

Prostate Problems Are Treatable — Not Just “Old Age”

Frequent night-time urination, weak stream, or incomplete emptying are usually caused by an enlarged prostate — and TURP, the endoscopic gold standard performed at Health1, resolves it through the natural passage with no external cut. Persistent symptoms deserve evaluation, not resignation.

When Kidneys Fail: A Transplant Program In-House

Because Health1 runs a kidney transplant program alongside its urology department, patients with advancing kidney disease get continuity — from stone disease and infections through dialysis planning to transplant evaluation — without changing hospitals. Common Questions

Do kidney stones always need surgery?

No. Small stones often pass with medication and hydration. Larger stones, stones causing blockage or infection, or stones that fail to pass need intervention — almost always endoscopic (through a keyhole or natural passage), not open surgery.

What is TURP and is it major surgery?

TURP (transurethral resection of the prostate) removes the obstructing part of an enlarged prostate through the natural urinary passage — no external incision. Most patients go home in 2–3 days with significant improvement in urinary symptoms.

Is blood in urine an emergency?

Visible blood in urine always needs evaluation, even if painless and even if it happens only once — it can signal stones, infection, or occasionally cancer. Book a urology consultation promptly rather than waiting for it to recur.

Is treatment cashless at Health1?

Yes, for insured patients — Health1 is NABH-accredited and empanelled with major insurers, TPAs, CGHS and PMJAY. Our insurance desk confirms your coverage before planned procedures.
