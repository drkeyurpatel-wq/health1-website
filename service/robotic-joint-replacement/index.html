Why Robotic Joint Replacement

Robotic-assisted joint replacement uses 3D planning and a surgical robot to place the implant with precision that's difficult to match freehand — better alignment tends to mean better long-term implant function and, for many patients, a faster return to walking. Health1 also offers Oxford-certified microplasty (partial knee replacement) for patients who don't need a full knee replacement.

Signs You May Be a Candidate

Joint pain limiting daily activities
Pain not relieved by medication or injections
Stiffness worse in the morning
Visible joint deformity
X-ray confirmed advanced arthritis

Joint replacement is generally considered after conservative options — physiotherapy, weight management, medication, injections — haven't provided lasting relief.

What to Expect: Pre-Surgery to Full Recovery

  • Pre-surgery: Imaging-based 3D planning specific to your joint anatomy, plus routine fitness-for-surgery checks.
  • Surgery day: Typically 60-90 minutes; most patients stand and take first steps with support the same day or next morning.
  • Hospital stay: Usually 2-4 days, depending on recovery pace and any other health factors.
  • Return to daily activity: Most patients walk without support indoors by 3-4 weeks, with continued strengthening over the following months.

Common Questions

How is robotic knee replacement different from conventional surgery?

The robot assists with precise bone preparation and implant alignment based on your specific anatomy, planned in advance from imaging. This tends to translate to more consistent alignment and, for many patients, faster early recovery.

Will I really walk the same day?

Most patients begin walking with a walker or support the same day or the day after surgery, as part of an early-mobilization recovery protocol — your specific timeline depends on your overall health and the complexity of your case.

What if I only need part of my knee replaced?

Partial knee replacement (microplasty) is an option for patients whose arthritis is limited to one part of the knee, preserving more of your natural joint — your surgeon will assess whether you're a candidate.
