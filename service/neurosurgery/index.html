Surgical and Interventional, Working Together

Some brain and spine conditions are best treated surgically; others respond better to minimally invasive, catheter-based interventional techniques; some need a combination of both. Health1's structure — neurosurgery working alongside interventional neurology under the same roof — means your case gets assessed by both perspectives rather than defaulting to whichever specialist you happened to see first.

Symptoms That Warrant Neurosurgical Evaluation

Progressive weakness in a limb
Persistent headaches with vomiting
New seizures
Vision changes with neurological symptoms
Loss of bladder/bowel control (seek emergency care)

From Referral to Surgery Decision

Most patients referred for a neurosurgical opinion do not end up needing surgery — many conditions are monitored or managed medically first. When surgery is discussed, it follows detailed imaging review and, often, a joint discussion with the interventional neurology team to confirm surgery is genuinely the better option for that specific case.

Common Questions

What's the difference between neurosurgery and interventional neurology?

Neurosurgery involves open or minimally invasive surgical procedures on the brain or spine. Interventional neurology treats certain conditions (like some aneurysms or strokes) through a catheter, without open surgery. Some conditions can be treated either way, and your case is assessed to determine the better fit.

What are warning signs of a brain tumor?

Persistent headaches (especially worsening or with vomiting), new seizures, vision changes, or progressive neurological symptoms like weakness or speech difficulty warrant prompt evaluation.

Is spine surgery the same as neurosurgery for the spine?

Both neurosurgeons and orthopaedic spine surgeons perform spine surgery in India — the specific condition and surgeon's training determine who's involved, and Health1's teams coordinate on complex cases.
